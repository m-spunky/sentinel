● SentinelAI Fusion — Plain English Breakdown                                                      
                                                                                                   
  ---
  What is it, in one sentence?                                                                        
  It's a cybersecurity command center — you paste in a suspicious email or URL, and AI instantly   
  tells you if it's an attack, who's behind it, how confident it is, and what to do next.          

  ---
  The Real-World Problem It Solves

  Imagine you work at a bank. Every day:
  - Employees get thousands of emails
  - Some are phishing attacks (fake "your account is locked" emails)
  - Attackers also create fake websites that look exactly like your bank's login page
  - One click from one employee = entire company compromised

  Traditional tools check one thing at a time (just the link, or just the email text). They miss   
  attacks that look normal in isolation but are obviously malicious when you look at the full      
  picture.

  SentinelAI looks at everything simultaneously and connects the dots.

  ---
  The 5-Page Frontend — What Each Page Does

  1. Landing Page (/)

  What it is: The marketing/home page a potential customer sees first.

  Components:
  - Navbar — Top bar with logo + "Launch Console" CTA
  - LandingHero — The big headline + animated stats ("99.2% detection rate")
  - ProblemSection — Explains WHY existing tools fail (three pain points)
  - SolutionSection — Shows how Fusion's layered approach works differently
  - FeaturesGrid — Four capability cards: multi-modal detection, threat graph, AI explanations,    
  chat assistant
  - ProcessFlow — Step-by-step pipeline visualization (Input → Neural Analysis → Fusion → Intel →  
  Response)
  - ComparisonSection — Table: SentinelAI vs legacy tools
  - FinalCTA — "Start now" button
  - LandingFooter — Links, social, copyright

  Real-world use: A CISO (Chief Information Security Officer) visits, reads it, and decides to buy 
  it for their company.

  ---
  2. Dashboard (/dashboard)

  What it is: The SOC (Security Operations Center) overview — a live TV screen showing your        
  security health right now.

  Components:
  - KpiCards — 4 big numbers at the top:
    - Total threats detected today
    - High-risk incidents
    - Active campaigns being tracked
    - Average AI confidence score
  - ThreatTimeline (Recharts AreaChart) — A line graph showing threat volume over the last 24      
  hours, split by type (phishing in red, malware in amber, behavioral anomalies in blue). Like a   
  stock chart but for attacks.
  - RiskDonut (Recharts PieChart) — A donut chart showing your current risk breakdown. Center shows
   total score.
  - ThreatFeed — A live, real-time feed of new threats as they're detected, connected via WebSocket
   (like a live Twitter feed but for attacks). Has a green "Live" dot when connected.
  - ThreatChart — Spinning risk ring showing overall platform health (78% elevated risk).

  Real-world use: A security analyst arrives at work, opens dashboard, immediately sees "3 new     
  high-risk phishing campaigns detected overnight" and knows what to prioritize.

  ---
  3. Analyze Page (/dashboard/analyze)

  What it is: The actual detection tool — where you submit a suspicious email/URL for AI analysis. 

  Components:
  - AnalysisInputSection — Three tabs: paste email text, paste URL, or paste raw email headers. You
   hit "Run Analysis" and the 5-model pipeline fires.
  - DetectionPipelineCard — Shows the 5 analysis stages in real-time with scores:
    a. Pre-Process (sanitize input)
    b. NLP Engine (GPT-4o-mini reads the text for phishing language)
    c. URL Analyzer (checks domain age, DNS records, URLhaus blacklists)
    d. Visual Engine (takes screenshot of site, compares it to real brand logos via CLIP)
    e. Fusion (combines all 4 scores into one final verdict)
  - ThreatScoreCard — The big circular gauge showing final threat percentage (0–100%). Red =       
  phishing, amber = suspicious, blue = clean.
  - ModelBreakdown — Four horizontal bars showing what each AI model individually scored. You can  
  see "NLP said 89% suspicious, but URL said 20%" — useful for understanding why.
  - ShapChart — Bar chart showing WHICH features drove the score. Example: "Urgency language       
  (+34%), Domain age 2 days (+28%), No DMARC record (+15%)". This is how you explain to a
  non-technical manager WHY the AI flagged it.
  - TacticsCard — Maps the attack to MITRE ATT&CK framework (the industry-standard catalog of      
  attack techniques). Example: "T1566 — Spearphishing Attachment".
  - IntelCard — Shows if this email is connected to a known campaign (e.g., "This is part of       
  Operation Wire Phantom by FIN7").
  - ExplanationCard — GPT-4o-mini writes a human-readable forensic paragraph: "This email exhibits 
  classic credential harvesting techniques. The sender domain was registered 2 days ago and mimics 
  PayPal's visual identity..."
  - Rapid Incident Response buttons — One-click actions: Quarantine, Block IOCs, Alert Team,       
  Generate PDF Report.

  Real-world use: Employee forwards a suspicious email to the security team → analyst pastes it    
  here → 400ms later has a full forensic breakdown → clicks "Quarantine" and "Alert Team".

  ---
  4. Intelligence Page (/dashboard/intelligence)

  What it is: The threat actor research center — like a detective's wall connecting all the dots.  

  Components:
  - GraphView (D3.js force graph) — An interactive network diagram. Nodes are:
    - Red = threat actors (FIN7, Lazarus Group)
    - Purple = campaigns (Operation Wire Phantom)
    - Blue = domains (auth-login.net)
    - Orange = IP addresses
    - Green = MITRE techniques

  Lines connect related nodes. You can drag, zoom, click a node to see details. This shows that the
   same actor is running multiple campaigns across multiple domains.
  - DetailsPanel — Right sidebar showing selected node's full profile: description, MITRE
  techniques, activity frequency chart, related entities list.
  - Search/Filter — Filter by actor, domain, IP, etc.

  Real-world use: After detecting an attack, an analyst clicks on the campaign node and sees: "FIN7
   ran 14 similar campaigns last month, targeting banking infrastructure in Eastern Europe."       

  ---
  5. Campaigns Page (/dashboard/campaigns)

  What it is: Case management — tracking ongoing attack operations over time.

  Components:
  - CampaignList — Left sidebar listing all tracked campaigns with status badges (Active = red     
  pulse, Investigating = amber, Mitigated = green). Click one to open it.
  - CampaignDetails — The full case file:
    - Campaign ID, status, actor, last activity
    - Stats: target sectors, IOC count, mapped techniques, confidence
    - IOC List (live from backend) — all malicious domains/IPs associated with this campaign. Click
   any to investigate.
    - Attack Velocity Chart — bar chart showing when attack activity spiked
    - MITRE technique badges
  - ActionPanel — Response buttons: Quarantine, Block Domains, Notify Team, Escalate L3
  - Historical Threat Landscape — Full-width bar chart showing attack frequency across a 6-month   
  timeline

  Real-world use: Your team is tracking a ransomware group. This page is the "war room" — all the  
  IOCs they've used, all their infrastructure, their timeline, updated live.

  ---
  6. Chat Page (/dashboard/chat) — SentinelChat

  What it is: An AI cybersecurity assistant that knows your entire platform's data.

  Components:
  - ChatHeader — Shows "SentinelChat Active" with inference status
  - ChatMessage — Each message rendered as a card. AI responses include clickable source badges and
   follow-up suggestion buttons.
  - ChatInput — The message box. Sends queries to GPT-4o via the backend RAG pipeline.
  - ContextPanel — Right sidebar showing currently active threat context (what entity the AI is    
  "looking at"), related IOCs, quick action buttons.

  Real-world use: You type: "What campaigns has FIN7 run targeting banking in 2026?" — the AI      
  searches your knowledge graph and returns a structured answer with links. Or: "Explain what T1566
   means in plain English" — it explains MITRE technique T1566 using your organization's context.
